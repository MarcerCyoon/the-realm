December 4, 2019
Meter
If I asked you to define a meter, you'll probably tell me it's a 100 centimeters, or about 1.1 yards. Maybe you'll spread out your arms and show me an approximation, or compare it to your height. And that's all fine for a casual conversation on length. That's not fine, though, for scientists trying to find a rigorous definition for the meter; as the SI unit for length, the sciences necessitate a more formal definition. So, in 1983, the meter became accurately defined through a universal constant: c, the speed of light. The meter is now the length of the path travelled by light in vacuum during a time interval of 1/299792458 of a second. In an interesting way, perhaps running counterintuitive to our early education, the speed of light is not determined in terms of meters -- the meter is defined in terms of the speed of light.
December 3, 2019
Stuck In The Sky
Tomorrow morning, I'll be in an airplane, on my way back to the United States. My flight's going to be around 13 hours. That's a long, long time to be stuck on an airplane thousands of feet in the air, in a seat too cramped and too small. My intense flight got me curious about what the longest commercial passenger flight, time-wise, was. It turns out just a few months ago a Qantas airplane flew from New York to Syndey for just over 19 hours. That's a record for longest nonstop commercial passenger flight. It seems to mostly be a one-off experimental flight, though. The longest flight offered is Singapore Airline's Singapore-Newark flight, which takes a whopping 18 hours and 30 minutes.
Boy am I suddenly happy that I'm trapped on an airplane for only 13 hours. Well, time to catch up on reading We Need to Talk About Kevin and play some more Super Smash Bros. Melee!

December 1, 2019
Commands Command Prompt Cannot Understand
Like any normal person does, I spent the first day of December mostly on my computer. Recently, I had my almost five-year-old MacBook Pro break down on me, so I've been borrowing a Lenovo Thinkpad until my laptop arrives. I opened Command Prompt to set up a few repos on the new computer when, to my surprise, the 'ls' command refused to work. A quick Google search yields the answer: 'ls' is not a recognized command in Command Prompt! It's only a Unix command. You have to use the equivalent 'dir' while working in Command Prompt. As someone who's used Terminal for their entire programming career, this was a pretty neat new thing to learn.
Computer scientists and their love for inconsistencies, am I right?
